## 1. Incorporating Your Business

File an LLC immediately through your state's secretary of state website (most affordable) or via Stripe Atlas (faster, more streamlined, but pricier). An LLC separates your personal assets from business assets in the event of a lawsuit.

Use your tax ID to open a business bank account and business credit card. Keep business and personal funds completely separate — the IRS doesn't like to see them mixed.

### Choosing a Business Name

Don't use your personal name. Operate under a business name to remove unconscious bias and better represent a growing team. Adding "Web Design" to the end helps with SEO. Using your town name (e.g. "Oak Harbor Web Designs") is even better for local search.

Examples of good naming approaches:
- Location + Web Design: "Oak Harbor Web Designs"
- Region + Web Design: "Great Lakes Web Design"
- Personal connection: "High Seas Web Design", "Compass Web Design"

### How to Do Taxes

Hire a local accountant. Use an app like **Everlance** to track mileage, income, and expenses — connect your bank account and credit card and it categorizes everything automatically.

Open two business bank accounts: one main account, one where you set aside 30% for taxes. Pay quarterly estimated taxes to avoid a large bill at year end.

## 2. Sending Invoices and Managing Clients

Recommended invoicing tools:
- **Square Up** (US) — supports automatic recurring invoices, client saves card on file
- **Bonsai** (international) — available in more countries

### Automatic Invoicing

Set all monthly subscription invoices to go out on the **1st of the month**. If a client signs mid-month, charge a prorated amount for that month and set the recurring invoice to start on the 1st of the following month. This keeps all billing on the same day.

### Domain Management

Buy domains for clients rather than having them manage it themselves. Use **Porkbun** (avoid GoDaddy). Charge $20/year, set up a yearly recurring invoice. You own and control the domain — safer for the client, easier for you.

### Personal Touch

Store each client's address in your invoicing software. Send personalized holiday cards. Small details build lasting relationships.

## 3. Your Business Model & Pricing

Two options: **subscription** or **lump sum**. Offering both is ideal.

### Lump Sum Pricing

Standard 5-page static site: **$3,000 USD** + $25/month hosting. Edits billed at $50/hour with a 1-hour minimum.

When starting out, charge competitively ($1,200 or less) and raise rates as your quality improves. Price based on the value of your work, not hours alone.

To set your rate: take your comfortable hourly wage, double or triple it, multiply by 30 hours (high-end estimate for a 5-page site).

### Subscription Pricing

**$0 down, $150/month** — includes hosting, unlimited edits, 24/7 support, lifetime updates, analytics, and Google Business Profile help.

Key benefits of this model:
- Predictable residual income
- Low financial risk for client
- Every new subscriber = $1,800/year raise

6-month minimum contract, then month-to-month.

**How to sell it:** "Because I handle everything for you. Your time is worth money, and every minute you spend fiddling with a website is a minute you're not running your business. At $150/month you have a personal web developer in your pocket, 24/7."

### E-commerce

Always lump sum for e-commerce — no subscriptions. Requires a Shopify developer partner. Cost breakdown example:
- Your fee: $3,000
- Design: $500
- Shopify dev: $1,500–$2,000
- Project management markup: $500
- **Total: ~$5,500 minimum**

## 4. Templates – The Key To Success

Build reusable templates. This is how you scale.

- Start with 3 templates: casual/simple, modern/professional, luxury
- Sell each template once per market area (never reuse in the same market)
- Track which templates are in which markets using a custom Google Map
- Interior pages (About, Services, Contact, etc.) can be reused across all sites — only build a new home page per template

Once you have a starter base kit, new templates go from 20-30 hours to 6-10 hours.

**Reusable section libraries can compress this to hours instead of days** — clean, accessible, hand-coded components ready to drop in.

### Reuse Interior Page Templates

Nobody cares about unique interior pages. Build a collection of About, Services, Portfolio, Testimonials, and Contact page templates and rotate between them. Only invest time in building a unique home page per design.

## 5. Building A Team

Start building a team early — don't try to do everything yourself.

### Designer

Find designers on **Dribbble** or **Behance**, or post on the r/web_design subreddit. Scope the work to home page only (desktop, no mobile) in Figma. Budget: $250–$300 per home page design.

If you don't have a designer yet, buy themes from **Themeforest**, purchase a license, and rebuild in clean HTML/CSS.

### Copywriter

Content is critical for SEO. Find copywriters on **Fiverr** — look for those with journalism, English, communications, or marketing degrees and experience in SEO content. Budget: $0.10–$0.15/word (~$150–$200 for a home page). Have 1–2 on tap so you can run multiple projects simultaneously.

### Logo Designer

Get SVGs — they're lightweight (10–20KB vs 70–150KB+ for PNG) and scale infinitely. Find a logo designer on Fiverr who can vectorize client logos to SVG for ~$20.

### E-commerce Developer

Find Shopify developers on **Upwork**. Best talent pools at reasonable rates: Ukraine, Poland, Czech Republic (~$30–$50/hour vs $150/hour US). Always lump sum for these projects.

### Back-end Developer

Find on Upwork, Reddit (r/webdev), LinkedIn, or local dev meetups. Useful for custom applications (quote calculators, dashboards, etc.).

### Ads, SEO & Marketing

Don't try to do this yourself. Partner with:
- A Google Ads specialist
- An SEO team
- A social media/marketing person

Refer clients to these partners. Collect referral fees where possible.

## 6. Contracts

**Always use a contract.** Have a lawyer write two: one for lump sum, one for subscriptions. Budget ~$800.

### Essential Contract Clauses

1. Price, payment terms, and extras (hosting, domain, Shopify fees)
2. You have final say on design
3. Cancellation clauses (non-payment, harassment, late payment)
4. No liability for damages or lawsuits resulting from the site
5. For subscriptions: late payment fees, 6-month minimum, cancellation = client owes full site price
6. Client cannot reuse your code or design elsewhere (temporary license only)
7. All disputes handled in your county/state court

### E-Signatures

Use **Adobe Sign** ($27/month) or **DocuSign**. Process:
1. Fill out contract in Word, print to PDF
2. Upload to Adobe Sign, add recipient email
3. Place signature fields, click Send
4. Receive notifications when viewed and signed

## 7. How To Find Clients

### Pre-Qualifying Leads

Don't call everyone — pre-qualify to maximize your conversion rate.

**What to look for when scanning websites:**

1. **Who made it?** Check the footer. If it's a marketing company, skip — they're likely under contract.
2. **Visual check** — Does it look professional with 10+ pages? Skip. Too hard a sell.
3. **No website?** Google them to confirm, then add to your list.
4. **Reviews** — Do they have recent (within last year) good reviews? Do they respond professionally to bad ones? No reviews in 3+ years = skip.
5. **Site review** — Use **BuiltWith** to check what it's built with. Note page speed. Check for thin content.
6. **Google/GoDaddy builder sites** — Immediate call. These owners know their site is bad and are primed to upgrade.

### Building Your Lead Spreadsheet

Columns: Business Name, Web Address, Phone, # of calls, Search Location, Business Type, Notes, Page Speed.

Color code rows: Orange = called, Blue = interested, Green = sold, Black = do not call.

### Process

1. Google Maps search for your target industry in an area
2. Open 20+ websites in new tabs
3. Pre-qualify each one using the criteria above
4. Add qualifying leads to spreadsheet
5. Repeat on Yelp for the same area
6. Aim for 50+ leads before calling

Call businesses once every 3 days, weekdays only. After 3 unanswered calls, leave a voicemail and mark as done.

### Sales Stats

Out of ~100 calls: 30–40 answer, 7–10 interested, 2–3 close.

### Start Local

No portfolio yet? Start local so potential clients can meet you in person. Build your local portfolio first, then expand.

### Networking

Connect with:
- Back-end developers who need a front-end partner
- SEO and marketing people who need a developer
- Other agencies who have overflow work

Post your work on Reddit, LinkedIn. Attend local dev meetups.

## 8. How To Make Sales Calls

### The Opening

Don't sound like a salesman. Don't ask how they are. Get to the point.

**Opening script:**
> "Hey, is this [Business Name]?"
> 
> *(They confirm)*
> 
> "Awesome, my name is Ryan and I'm a [stay-at-home dad / freelance] web developer. I found you on Google, poked around your site, and saw it's a fairly standard WordPress site that could use some work. I wanted to call and see if I could help make you something better."

**If they have no website:**
> "I found you on Google but didn't see a website anywhere, so I wanted to at least call and see if you needed help with one."

### The Pitch

When they ask about price, lead with the subscription model:

> "I do things a little different — $0 down and $150/month. That includes hosting, unlimited edits, 24/7 support, lifetime updates, analytics, help with your Google Profile — everything. I do it all for you. 6-month minimum, month-to-month after that, cancel anytime."

### When They Ask "Why Should I Pay Monthly?"

> "Think about it this way — if the website brings in just one new customer a month, it more than pays for itself. That $150/month isn't just the website. It's access to me. It's peace of mind. I'm here for you when you need me, so you can spend your time running your business instead of worrying about your website."

### When They Ask "What Do You Do That's Better?"

Cover these points in a natural conversation (not a script):

1. I custom code everything — no page builders
2. Google's Core Vitals update rewards fast, clean code
3. Page builders have bloated, hackable code that scores 34/100
4. My sites score 98–100 — as fast as they can possibly be
5. Every second of load time = lost customers
6. I use website conversion funnels to structure content for conversions
7. I hire a copywriter for keyword-rich, engaging content

### Key Principles

- Let them lead the conversation — don't use loaded questions
- If they're busy, ask when to call back — don't pressure them
- Be honest about who you are and how you found them
- Talk like a friend, not a salesman
- Sell a relationship, not a website

### When They're Ready to Move Forward

Walk them through the process:
1. Send contract to e-sign
2. Send first invoice, then set up recurring invoice on the 1st
3. Send questionnaire about their business
4. Share design options for them to pick from
5. Designer creates mockup, you do a screen share review
6. Optional: loop in copywriter ($150 add-on)
7. You code it, send demo link for approval
8. Optimize, launch

## 9. Making Sales In Person

When you have no portfolio, walk into local businesses and talk directly to the owner. Being physically present builds trust that a cold call can't.

### How to Approach

Ask to speak with the owner. When they come out, introduce yourself and say you noticed some issues with their website and wanted to talk about how to fix them.

**Walk through specific problems you identified beforehand:**
- Thin content that won't rank on Google
- Poor page speed score (mention their actual score)
- Missing pages (Contact, Services, Reviews, FAQ)
- Outdated design

**Frame it as problem-solving, not selling.** You're there to help, not pitch.

**When they ask how you found them:**
> "I was looking at websites around town to see what's out there and what can be improved. I found your site, read your reviews — you're clearly good people and someone I'd like to work with. So I studied your site and put together a plan on how I'd help."

Key principles:
- Be authentic and honest
- Let them ask the questions, your answers become the pitch
- Don't use scripted sales tactics or loaded questions
- Sell yourself as a person and a relationship

## 10. Building Your Website

Start from a base kit every time — never from scratch. Use **cs-base** (this repo) or your own forked starter.

Use reusable HTML/CSS sections to compress development from 20–30 hours to a few hours per site.

### Using a component library for design (without a designer)

1. Open Figma and create a new frame at 2000px wide
2. Browse your section library, copy Figma design files for sections you want
3. Paste into your frame and stack sections vertically
4. Present to client on a screen share (Google Meet)
5. Save chosen stitches to a client folder in your library for easy code retrieval

### Working With a Designer

- Send them: client's current site, demo links of styles the client likes, page structure outline
- Scope to home page desktop design only in Figma (you handle mobile translation)
- Budget for subscriptions: $250–$300 per design
- Pay via ACH transfer for lowest fees

### Working With a Copywriter

- Send: Figma design link (with placeholder/dummy text by section) + client's current site
- They return a Google Doc organized by section — you copy/paste into the site
- Budget: ~$150–$200 per home page

### Presenting a Design

Use Google Meet screen share. Look presentable — this is your first face-to-face with the client. Walk through the design together and note any requested changes.

### Finishing Checklist

Before launch, complete every item:

1. Optimize all images and page speed (see `docs/WEB-DESIGN-RESOURCES.md` — Page Speed Handbook)
2. Validate HTML at https://validator.w3.org/
3. Check all links at https://www.drlinkcheck.com/ — zero errors
4. Run Lighthouse in Chrome DevTools — target 100 across all categories
5. Check browser console for red errors
6. Page speed test at https://pagespeed.web.dev/ — target 95–100
7. Generate sitemap at https://www.xml-sitemaps.com/ — add to root directory
8. Generate and add favicon at https://realfavicongenerator.net/
9. Add Google Analytics to `<head>` (bottom, above `<title>`)
10. Submit to Google Search Console
11. Test on all major devices via https://www.browserstack.com/
12. Add schema markup using https://technicalseo.com/tools/schema-markup-generator/

## 11. How To Manage a Project

**Project flow:**
1. Sign contract
2. Collect first payment (or 50% deposit for lump sum)
3. Client fills out questionnaire
4. Design phase
5. Copywriting (optional add-on)
6. Design approval via screen share
7. Build the site
8. Client reviews demo link
9. Optimize and launch

**Tracking:** Use a physical whiteboard. Write each client's name, project cost, amount paid, and next steps. Seeing everything at a glance without opening an app keeps you organized when running 5–8 projects simultaneously.

### Lump Sum Invoice Structure

In the invoice description, include: "50% deposit of $X total to be paid for website development." This makes payment history easy to reference later.

### Design Estimates

Send the client 5 Themeforest demo links matching the style they described. Ask which they like and why. Share that + their current site + their content with your designer and ask for an estimate.

**Pricing example:**
- Home page only design: charge client $500, designer costs ~$300
- 5-page design: charge client $1,500, designer costs ~$1,000–$1,200
- Add markup for your time as project manager

## 12. How To Get Your New Site Online

Before starting, confirm with the client:
1. Do they have domain login credentials?
2. Do they have @domain emails set up?

Buy the domain for them if they don't have one. Use **Porkbun**. Charge $20/year, set up a yearly recurring invoice.

### Hosting with Netlify

Use **Netlify** (https://netlify.com) — free for static sites, includes 100 form submissions/month per site, auto-deploys from GitHub.

**Setup steps:**
1. Add new site → Import from GitHub → Select repo → Deploy
2. Change site name to client's business name
3. Enable asset optimization (but **uncheck "Bundle CSS"** — it creates a large render-blocking file)
4. Add `data-netlify="true"` to your form tag
5. Set up email notifications for form submissions (your email + client's email)

### Connecting a Custom Domain

1. Site Settings → Domain Settings → Add custom domain
2. Set primary domain (with or without www)
3. Set up Netlify DNS
4. **Before switching nameservers:** copy all existing DNS records (especially MX, SPF, TXT for email) to a backup text file
5. Add all email-related DNS records to Netlify DNS
6. Copy Netlify's nameservers into the client's domain registrar (Porkbun → NS settings)
7. Delete old nameservers, enter the 4 Netlify nameservers, submit
8. Domain goes live within 72 hours as DNS propagates worldwide

Netlify auto-provisions SSL certificates every 6 months.
